# War Gaming

## Purpose

Pressure-test a strategy by simulating how the world will actually respond to it — competitors, customers, regulators, internal organization — and identify where the strategy breaks before reality does the breaking.

The output is not a confidence score. It is a sharper strategy: evolved by the war game, with response plans pre-built for the likely failure modes.

## Governing Principle

**The market does not stand still while you execute. Plan for the response, not just the move.**

Most strategies are built as if they will be executed against a passive opponent. War-gaming makes the opponent active.

## The Three Things a War Game Must Produce

1. **A revealed weakness** — at least one place where the strategy breaks under pressure
2. **A revealed strength** — a competitive advantage that survives even hostile response
3. **A revised strategy** — adjustments to the plan, made before launch, that account for what was learned

If a war game produces none of these, it was not a war game. It was a meeting.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the scenarios to test

A useful war game tests 3–5 scenarios. Choose them deliberately, not randomly:

- **Scenario 1 — Most likely competitor response:** what does the dominant competitor most plausibly do?
- **Scenario 2 — Worst-case competitor response:** what is the most damaging move a competitor could make?
- **Scenario 3 — Market shift:** a change in macro conditions, regulation, or customer behavior that erodes the strategy's foundation
- **Scenario 4 — Execution failure:** the strategy is right but the organization cannot deliver — what fails first?
- **Scenario 5 (optional) — Black swan:** a low-probability, high-impact event (entrant, technology, geopolitical)

Each scenario must be specific. "Competitors respond aggressively" is not a scenario. "Competitor X cuts price 15% on the top three SKUs in Q3" is a scenario.

### Step 2 — Form the teams

A war game requires at least four roles, played by people who can step out of their day-job perspective:

- **Blue team:** us — defending and executing the strategy
- **Red team:** the primary competitor(s) — playing to win, not to support our narrative
- **Yellow team:** the customer / market — making real buying decisions based on what each side does
- **Control:** facilitator, ruling on plausibility, enforcing time, surfacing what is being avoided

The single most important rule: the Red team must be staffed with people who genuinely want to win as the competitor would. Stocking the Red team with friendly skeptics who lightly poke holes is the most common war game failure.

### Step 3 — Brief each team on their actual incentives

The Red team must be briefed on:

- The competitor's stated strategy
- The competitor's financial pressures and shareholder demands
- The competitor's CEO compensation incentives
- The competitor's recent capital allocation pattern
- What the competitor's CEO would lose by NOT responding

This brief is the difference between a war game and a thought experiment. The Red team's plays must be plausible given what the competitor would actually optimize for.

### Step 4 — Run the moves in turns

Standard structure: 3 turns, each representing roughly 6–12 months of real time.

For each turn:

1. **Blue team announces its move** for the period (what it does, with what resources)
2. **Yellow team scores customer response** (who buys what, who switches, who stays)
3. **Red team responds**, briefing Blue on the competitive move
4. **Yellow team re-scores** based on the new state
5. **Control surfaces consequences** — second-order effects, capability strain, capital pressure

Document everything. The artifact of value is the move log, not the conclusion.

### Step 5 — Force second and third-order effects

Most war games stop at the first move. The interesting failures emerge in the second and third turns:

- Customers who initially supported the new strategy reconsider after watching competitor response
- The internal organization shows execution strain from sustaining the move
- Capital allocated to the primary play becomes unavailable for defense elsewhere
- The competitor's response triggers a response from a third competitor not initially modeled

Push the war game past comfort. The first turn is the strategy's best face. Turns two and three are where reality lives.

### Step 6 — Identify the failure modes

After the turns conclude, name the failures explicitly:

- **What broke?** Specifically, which assumption proved fragile under pressure?
- **What surprised us?** Where did the Red team play we did not anticipate?
- **Where did our advantage hold?** The places the strategy survived hostile response are the real moats.
- **Where did we underestimate cost?** Often the cost of *defending* the original move exceeds the cost of *making* it.

### Step 7 — Pre-commit responses to the realistic failure modes

For each plausible failure mode identified, define:

- **The early-warning indicator:** what we will see in the market data 60–90 days before the failure becomes obvious
- **The trigger threshold:** the metric level that initiates response, named in advance
- **The pre-committed response:** what we will do, with what resources, on what timeline
- **The commitment escalation point:** the point at which we double down or retreat (sunk-cost discipline matters here)

Pre-commitment is the war game's most valuable output. Without it, the lesson is forgotten the moment the room clears.

### Step 8 — Revise the strategy

The war game must change something about the strategy, or the exercise was theater. Three legitimate revisions:

1. **Sequencing change:** different order of moves to reduce exposure
2. **Capability change:** invest in a defensive capability before launching the offensive move
3. **Scope change:** narrower initial move to reduce signal to competitors and slow their response

Document what changed and why. The strategy that emerges from a war game should look measurably different from the one that entered it.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test During the Game

1. **"The competitor's response will be slower than we assume."** Stress this — most strategies assume comfortable response time and lose it.
2. **"Our customers will follow us through competitive turbulence."** Will they? Or will the noise drive them to alternatives?
3. **"Our organization can sustain the move at the pace required."** Or will execution fatigue erode the advantage?
4. **"The financial case holds even if the move takes 18 months longer than planned."** Most strategies' returns evaporate with timeline slippage. Test this.
5. **"We will recognize the failure mode early enough to respond."** Or will we wait for unambiguous evidence and then be too late?

## Quality Checks Before Concluding

- [ ] 3–5 scenarios defined with specific, plausible mechanics — not generic descriptors
- [ ] Red team genuinely played to win, not to validate Blue
- [ ] Red team briefed on competitor incentives, not just competitor public position
- [ ] At least 3 turns run, with second and third-order effects forced
- [ ] At least one weakness, one strength, and one revised strategy element identified
- [ ] Pre-committed responses written for each plausible failure mode
- [ ] Revised strategy is measurably different from the pre-game version

## Common Failure Modes

- **Friendly Red team.** Staffing the opposing team with people who soft-pedal. The whole exercise is wasted.
- **One-turn games.** Stopping at the first move; never seeing the cascade.
- **Confirming the strategy.** Running the war game to validate what leadership has already decided. War games that don't change anything are political theater.
- **Ignoring execution failure.** Modeling competitor response but assuming the organization executes flawlessly. This is the most under-modeled scenario.
- **Forgetting the customer.** Building elaborate competitor moves while assuming customers passively absorb them.
- **No pre-commitment.** Generating insights without translating them into pre-committed action thresholds. The lessons evaporate within weeks.

## Deliverable

A scenario brief (3–5 specific scenarios), a turn-by-turn move log across 3+ turns, a failure-mode register, pre-committed response thresholds for each plausible failure, and a revised strategy document showing what changed and why.
