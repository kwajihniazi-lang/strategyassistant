# Situation Assessment

## Purpose

Establish a single, defensible version of the truth about where the business stands today — across financials, market position, capabilities, and momentum — before any strategic recommendation is made.

A good situation assessment kills bad strategy before it starts. Most failed strategies trace back to a misdiagnosed starting point.

## Governing Principle

**Facts before frameworks. Frameworks before opinions. Opinions before recommendations.**

The associate's job is not to be clever. It is to be right about the starting point.

## The SCQA Frame (Pyramid Principle Setup)

Every situation assessment must answer four questions in sequence. Do not move on until each is locked.

1. **Situation** — What is objectively true about the business and its context? (No interpretation yet.)
2. **Complication** — What has changed, broken, or emerged that makes the current path insufficient?
3. **Question** — What is the one strategic question leadership now needs to answer?
4. **Answer** — What is the hypothesis we will test? (Held loosely until the data speaks.)

If the team cannot articulate the Complication crisply, the engagement has no anchor. Stop and re-interview the sponsor.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the unit of analysis (MECE)

Decompose the business into mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive segments. Choose ONE primary cut:

- By product line
- By customer segment
- By geography
- By channel
- By business unit / P&L

Pick the cut that maps to how decisions are actually made. Do not mix cuts in the primary view — that is the most common rookie error.

### Step 2 — Triangulate performance from three independent angles

Never trust a single data source. For each segment, build:

- **Financial lens:** revenue, margin, growth rate, capital intensity (last 3 years + forward forecast)
- **Market lens:** market size, share, share trend, relative position vs. top 3 competitors
- **Operational lens:** unit economics, productivity, cycle time, quality / NPS

If two lenses disagree, that disagreement is the insight. Investigate it before moving on.

### Step 3 — Build the performance map

Plot every segment on a 2x2: **growth rate (y) × profitability vs. peers (x)**. Annotate each bubble with revenue size.

This produces four quadrants the team must label honestly:
- **Engines** (high growth, high relative profit) — protect and feed
- **Drains** (low growth, low relative profit) — fix or exit
- **Cash cows** (low growth, high relative profit) — milk, do not over-invest
- **Question marks** (high growth, low relative profit) — diagnose: scaling pain or broken model?

### Step 4 — Test against the "outside view"

Pull the equivalent map for 3–5 peer companies using public data. Two checks:

- Is our performance distribution normal for this industry, or anomalous?
- Are we losing share in segments where peers are winning? (This is the true alarm bell.)

### Step 5 — Issue tree the gap

For every segment classified as Drain or Question Mark, build an issue tree to root cause. Rule: every branch must be MECE, every leaf must be testable with available data.

```
Underperformance in Segment X
├── Revenue problem
│   ├── Volume (price-holding, losing units)
│   └── Price (volume-holding, eroding price)
├── Cost problem
│   ├── COGS (input costs, mix, scale)
│   └── SG&A (productivity, allocation)
└── Capital problem
    ├── Working capital intensity
    └── Capex requirement vs. return
```

### Step 6 — Write the one-page situation summary

Format, non-negotiable:
- **Top line:** the single sentence that answers "where are we?"
- **Three supporting points:** each backed by one chart or one number
- **Implication:** what this means for the strategic question on the table

If it does not fit on one page, the diagnosis is not yet sharp enough.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

A senior associate enters every situation assessment with these four hypotheses already running. Disprove or confirm each:

1. **"The reported numbers overstate health."** Are we capitalizing costs, deferring investment, or pulling forward demand?
2. **"The market is moving faster than we are."** Is our share trend negative even where absolute revenue is growing?
3. **"Our averages hide the real story."** Does segment-level data reveal performance the consolidated P&L is masking?
4. **"The team's mental model is one cycle out of date."** What did leadership believe two years ago that is no longer true?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Every number has a source and a date
- [ ] Every chart answers a question — not just displays data
- [ ] The MECE decomposition does not double-count or leave gaps
- [ ] The "so what" is stated in plain English, not consulting jargon
- [ ] A skeptical CFO could not poke a hole in the top-line claim
- [ ] The summary leads with the answer (pyramid principle), not the journey

## Common Failure Modes

- **Boiling the ocean.** Cutting the business 5 ways at once. Pick one primary cut.
- **Single-source bias.** Trusting management's narrative without external triangulation.
- **Confirming the brief.** Finding what the sponsor already believes. Senior associates actively hunt for the disconfirming fact.
- **Burying the lead.** Writing a chronological journey instead of leading with the answer.

## Deliverable

A one-page situation summary, an annotated performance map, and a populated issue tree for every underperforming segment.
