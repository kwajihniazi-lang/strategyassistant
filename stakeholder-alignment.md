# Stakeholder Alignment

## Purpose

Identify the people whose support, neutrality, or active opposition will determine whether a strategy gets resourced and executed — and design the engagement plan that gets each one to the position the strategy needs.

The output is not a stakeholder list. It is a sequenced engagement plan that puts the strategy in the strongest position before any formal decision meeting.

## Governing Principle

**The decision is made before the meeting. The meeting only ratifies it.**

Senior associates do not present strategies cold. They build the consensus first, walk the room second.

## The Three Failure Modes the Audit Prevents

1. **Wrong room** — presenting to people without authority while the real decision-makers were unaligned
2. **Wrong message** — pitching the same story to everyone instead of tailoring to motivations
3. **Wrong sequence** — surprising a critical stakeholder in the meeting instead of engaging them in advance

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Map the stakeholder universe

List every individual (not function, not role — individuals by name) who will:

- Make the decision (vote yes or no)
- Influence the decision (advise the deciders)
- Execute on the decision (make it real)
- Be affected by the decision (gain or lose from it)

Aim for 10–25 named individuals on a typical strategic initiative. Fewer than 10 and the map is incomplete.

### Step 2 — Score on influence × interest

For each stakeholder, score:

- **Influence:** how much weight does their view carry in the decision? (H/M/L)
- **Interest:** how much do they personally care about the outcome? (H/M/L)

Plot on a 2x2:

- **High influence × High interest (key players):** these people decide the outcome. Engage them first, deeply, individually.
- **High influence × Low interest (latent power):** dangerous. They can derail with a casual remark. Inform them; do not require deep engagement, but do not surprise them.
- **Low influence × High interest (passionate base):** they care. They will champion or oppose loudly. Use them as advocates if aligned.
- **Low influence × Low interest (background):** monitor; do not invest engagement capital here.

Effort allocation: 60% on key players, 25% on latent power, 10% on passionate base, 5% on background.

### Step 3 — Diagnose each key player's actual position

For every High-Influence stakeholder, write:

- **Stated position:** what have they said publicly about this topic?
- **Actual position (your read):** what do you believe they really think?
- **Underlying motivation:** what are they personally optimizing for? (career, P&L, legacy, risk aversion, peer status)
- **Decision criteria:** what would cause them to support? what would cause them to oppose?
- **Information they trust:** who do they listen to? what kind of evidence persuades them? (data person vs. story person vs. peer-validation person)

The motivation analysis is the work. Most stakeholder maps stop at the position. The senior associate goes deeper to the *why*.

### Step 4 — Identify the swing votes and the blockers

From the key players list, separate into four buckets:

- **Champions:** already aligned, will actively support — leverage them; do not waste engagement time persuading
- **Supporters:** aligned but passive — give them ammunition to advocate
- **Swing votes:** undecided or directionally negative but movable — this is where the work happens
- **Blockers:** structurally opposed; their interests conflict with the strategy — must be either neutralized, repositioned, or routed around

The strategy lives or dies on the swing votes. Most stakeholder time should go to them.

### Step 5 — Tailor the message per stakeholder

Build a one-page "engagement card" per swing vote and key blocker:

- **Their question:** what is the question they walk into the room with?
- **Your one-line answer:** the headline that addresses their question directly
- **Their three concerns:** what will they object to? what risks will they raise?
- **Your prepared response to each concern:** anticipated, not improvised
- **The ask:** what specific commitment do you want from them by the end of the conversation?

The same strategy presented three different ways to three different stakeholders is not inconsistency — it is competence. The substance does not change. The framing does.

### Step 6 — Sequence the pre-alignment meetings

Order matters. Two rules:

- **Influence flows downhill:** align the highest-influence stakeholder first, then use their buy-in (with permission) to influence the next tier
- **Hardest conversations first:** if there is a blocker who could veto, surface that conversation early. Do not let it ambush the formal meeting.

Build a Gantt of pre-alignment conversations leading up to the formal decision date. Most strategies need 2–4 weeks of pre-alignment before a board or executive committee meeting.

### Step 7 — Run the pre-mortem on the meeting itself

Before the formal decision meeting, walk through:

- **Who speaks first?** That voice anchors the room.
- **What is the worst question that could be asked, and who would ask it?**
- **Who in the room has private concerns we have not addressed?**
- **What information could surface during the meeting that contradicts our story?**
- **What is the fallback if we sense the room is moving against us?**

A senior associate never enters a decision meeting with a single plan. They enter with a primary plan, a fallback position, and a knowing of which stakeholders will support each.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The named decision-maker is not the actual decision-maker."** Who does the named decider call before they decide?
2. **"The loudest opponent is not the most dangerous opponent."** The quiet skeptic with influence is the bigger threat.
3. **"Stated objections mask the real objection."** The technical concern is often a proxy for a political or career concern.
4. **"Alignment in private will not survive disagreement in public."** Has each stakeholder been pressure-tested in a setting where they had to defend their position?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Stakeholders mapped by name, not by role
- [ ] Influence × interest 2x2 produced
- [ ] Each key player has a written motivation analysis
- [ ] Swing votes and blockers explicitly named
- [ ] Per-stakeholder engagement card written for each swing vote
- [ ] Pre-alignment sequence has dates and a logical order
- [ ] Pre-mortem run on the formal decision meeting

## Common Failure Modes

- **Confusing org chart with influence chart.** Authority does not equal influence. Map the real network.
- **Presenting cold.** Bringing a strategy to a formal meeting before pre-aligning the swing votes.
- **One-message-fits-all.** Refusing to tailor framing because "the strategy is the strategy." Senior associates know substance and framing are different things.
- **Avoiding the blocker.** Hoping a blocker will stay quiet. They never do. Have the hard conversation early.
- **Confusing politeness with alignment.** A stakeholder who did not object is not the same as a stakeholder who will actively support.

## Deliverable

A named stakeholder map, an influence × interest 2x2, motivation analyses on key players, engagement cards per swing vote, a sequenced pre-alignment plan, and a written pre-mortem of the formal decision meeting.
