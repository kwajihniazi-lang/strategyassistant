# Competitive Intel

## Purpose

Build a fact-based, predictive view of the competitive landscape: who matters, what they are capable of, what they are likely to do next, and which of their moves would most damage our position.

The output is not a competitor profile deck. It is a working hypothesis about the next 12–24 months of competitive dynamics.

## Governing Principle

**Capabilities predict moves. Moves predict outcomes. Profiles predict nothing.**

A senior associate does not catalog competitors. They model them.

## The Three Questions Every Competitive Analysis Must Answer

1. **Who can hurt us?** — Not the loudest competitors, the most structurally dangerous ones.
2. **What will they do next?** — Based on their incentives, capabilities, and recent capital allocation, not their press releases.
3. **What is our winning response?** — Pre-committed, before the move happens.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the relevant competitive set (MECE)

Avoid the standard mistake of listing every company in the industry. Build three concentric rings:

- **Ring 1 — Direct competitors:** same customer, same problem, same solution shape
- **Ring 2 — Substitutes:** same customer, same problem, different solution
- **Ring 3 — Adjacent threats:** different customer or solution today, but with capabilities that could cross over

Most disruption comes from Ring 3. Most management attention goes to Ring 1. Allocate analytical effort accordingly.

### Step 2 — Apply Porter's Five Forces (rigorously, not ceremonially)

For each force, score Low / Medium / High and write one sentence of evidence. No evidence, no score.

- **Rivalry intensity:** concentration, growth rate, exit barriers, differentiation
- **Buyer power:** concentration, switching cost, price sensitivity
- **Supplier power:** concentration, substitutability, forward-integration risk
- **Threat of entry:** capital requirements, brand moats, regulatory barriers, network effects
- **Threat of substitutes:** relative price-performance, switching cost, buyer propensity

Then ask the question Porter himself emphasized: **which force is changing fastest, and in whose favor?**

### Step 3 — Build the capability map

For each Ring 1 and 2 competitor, score capability across the dimensions that matter in this industry. Use a 1–5 scale and force-rank — no ties.

| Capability | Us | Comp A | Comp B | Comp C |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Product / tech | | | | |
| Distribution reach | | | | |
| Cost position | | | | |
| Brand strength | | | | |
| Talent density | | | | |
| Capital access | | | | |
| Speed to market | | | | |

The pattern matters more than the scores. Look for:
- Where we are uniquely strong (defend)
- Where we are uniquely weak (urgent)
- Where the field is undifferentiated (opportunity to break out)

### Step 4 — Read their incentives, not their statements

For each priority competitor, answer:

- **Capital allocation:** where have they put the last $100M? (10-Ks, M&A, capex announcements)
- **Hiring signal:** what roles are they recruiting at scale? (LinkedIn, job boards)
- **Customer signal:** what are their customers saying changed in the last 6 months?
- **Patent / R&D signal:** where is the IP flow concentrated?
- **Compensation incentive:** what is the CEO paid to do? (proxy statement)

Write a one-paragraph "behavioral thesis" per competitor. What are they optimizing for, and what does that force them to do?

### Step 5 — War-game the next 4 moves

For each of the top 2–3 competitors, predict:

- **Move 1 (next 6 months):** the obvious play, given current trajectory
- **Move 2 (6–12 months):** the capability-extension play
- **Move 3 (12–24 months):** the strategic-shift play
- **Move 0 (the wildcard):** what would they do that we are not expecting?

For each move, score: probability (H/M/L) × impact on us (H/M/L). Focus only on the High × High and High × Medium cells.

### Step 6 — Pre-commit our responses

For every High-impact predicted move, define:
- The early-warning indicator (what we will see first)
- The trigger threshold (when we act, not when we discuss acting)
- The pre-committed response (what we do, with what resources, on what timeline)

Pre-commitment is the entire point. Strategies fail in the gap between "we saw it coming" and "we did something about it."

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The competitor we fear most is not the competitor we should fear most."** Is leadership pattern-matching to yesterday's threat?
2. **"Their public narrative differs from their capital allocation."** What are they doing vs. what are they saying?
3. **"A non-competitor today has the capabilities to be the dominant competitor in 24 months."** Who is closer than we think?
4. **"Industry structure is changing in a way that rewards a different business model than ours."** Are we defending the right hill?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Every competitive claim is backed by primary or near-primary evidence (not press recap)
- [ ] The Five Forces analysis identifies which force is moving, not just current state
- [ ] Capability scores are force-ranked — no fence-sitting
- [ ] Each predicted move has a probability AND an impact estimate
- [ ] Each high-impact move has a pre-committed response, not a "we should consider"
- [ ] A competitor's CFO would recognize the description of their own company

## Common Failure Modes

- **Mirror imaging.** Assuming competitors weigh the same trade-offs we do. They don't — their incentives differ.
- **Press-release strategy.** Treating announcements as commitments. Watch capital, not communications.
- **Static snapshot.** Producing a point-in-time view with no prediction. The deck is obsolete the day it ships.
- **Symmetric paranoia.** Treating all competitors as equal threats. Force-rank or the analysis is useless.

## Deliverable

A competitive landscape one-pager (Five Forces + ring map), a force-ranked capability matrix, behavioral theses for top 3 competitors, and a pre-committed response playbook for the top 5 predicted moves.
