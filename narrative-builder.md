# Narrative Builder

## Purpose

Convert strategic content into a narrative that lands the recommendation in the first 60 seconds, supports it with the minimum sufficient evidence, and survives hostile questioning.

A well-built strategy with a poor narrative gets killed in the room. The narrative is not decoration. It is the strategy's survival mechanism.

## Governing Principle

**Lead with the answer. Defend with structure. Detail only on demand.**

This is the Pyramid Principle, named: structure your communication so the most senior person in the room can make the decision in 60 seconds, while the most skeptical person in the room can drill into any branch and find rigor.

## The Three Tests Every Narrative Must Pass

1. **The headline test** — if the audience reads only the first slide / first paragraph, do they know the recommendation and the reasoning?
2. **The structure test** — does every supporting point either prove the headline or get cut?
3. **The defense test** — for every supporting point, can you show the evidence one level deeper without losing flow?

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Anchor the SCQA

Before writing anything, lock the four corners:

- **Situation:** what is true today that the audience already knows? (Common ground; one paragraph max.)
- **Complication:** what has changed or is changing that creates the need for a decision now?
- **Question:** what is the single question the audience needs answered?
- **Answer:** what is the recommendation? (One sentence; no hedging language.)

If the SCQA does not fit on a single page, the strategic thinking underneath is not yet sharp enough. Stop writing and rebuild the diagnosis.

### Step 2 — Build the governing thought

The governing thought is the one-sentence answer to the Question. It must satisfy three conditions:

- **It is an answer**, not a topic. ("We should enter market X" — yes. "Market X strategy" — no.)
- **It is debatable.** If everyone would already agree, it is not a strategic recommendation.
- **It is specific.** Vague answers cannot be defended.

Strong governing thoughts often follow this pattern: *"To achieve [outcome], we should [action] by [mechanism], delivering [quantified result] by [date]."*

### Step 3 — Build the supporting pyramid (MECE)

Below the governing thought, write the 3 (rarely 4, never more than 5) supporting arguments that, taken together, make the governing thought true.

The supporting arguments must be:

- **Mutually exclusive:** no overlap between arguments
- **Collectively exhaustive:** together they fully justify the governing thought
- **Parallel in form:** all action statements, or all reasons, or all conditions — not mixed

Test each supporting argument with the question: **"If this argument turned out to be wrong, would the governing thought still hold?"** If yes, the argument is not load-bearing — cut it. If no, it stays.

```
Governing thought: We should enter Market X by Q3 via partnership.
├── Supporting argument 1: The market is large enough to matter. (Sizing)
├── Supporting argument 2: We have a credible right to win. (Capability)
└── Supporting argument 3: Partnership beats build or buy on time and cost. (Approach)
```

### Step 4 — Populate evidence at the third level

Under each supporting argument, place 2–4 evidence points. These are the facts, charts, and analyses that prove the supporting argument. Same MECE / parallelism rules.

This third level is where the data, the analysis, the customer quotes, and the financials live. The audience should never need to go below this level — but the level should exist for when they do.

### Step 5 — Write the Day-1 slide

The Day-1 slide is the single page that, if everything else were lost, would still convey the recommendation. It contains:

- **Title:** the governing thought, written as a complete sentence (not "Q3 Strategy" but "We recommend entering Market X via partnership in Q3 to capture $40M revenue by 2027")
- **Three supporting points:** the second-level pyramid, each in one sentence
- **The "ask":** what specific decision or commitment is being requested?
- **The "so what if no":** the cost of inaction or alternative path

Most strategies fail in the room because the Day-1 slide does not exist. The audience has to wait 30 slides to understand what is being asked.

### Step 6 — Stress-test with the "but why" attack

Take the governing thought. Have someone ask "but why?" three times in a row. Each layer of the pyramid should answer one round of "but why?"

- *We should enter Market X via partnership.* — But why?
- *Because we have a credible right to win that we cannot exercise alone.* — But why?
- *Because the customer access we need is held by 3 incumbents we would otherwise compete with.* — But why?
- *Because direct entry would trigger price war that destroys segment economics for everyone.*

If the pyramid cannot survive three rounds of "but why," the structure is not deep enough. Rebuild.

### Step 7 — Compress to four communication formats

A senior associate prepares the same narrative in four lengths:

- **The 30-second version:** the governing thought + the headline number
- **The 2-minute version:** the SCQA + the three supporting arguments
- **The 10-minute version:** the full pyramid with evidence at the third level
- **The 60-minute version:** all of the above plus appendix-grade detail

The audience determines which version gets delivered, often without warning. Have all four ready.

### Step 8 — Engineer for the hostile question

For every supporting argument, prepare:

- **The two hardest questions** that could be asked
- **The one-sentence answer** to each
- **The backup chart or data** that proves the answer

A senior associate's calm under pressure is not personality. It is preparation.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test on Your Own Narrative

1. **"My governing thought is a topic, not an answer."** Read it aloud — does it make a claim, or just name a subject?
2. **"My three supporting arguments are not actually MECE."** Is there overlap, or a gap that would let a skeptic drive a wedge?
3. **"I am leading with the journey, not the answer."** Does the deck open with context-setting that buries the recommendation?
4. **"My evidence proves my supporting points, but my supporting points do not actually prove my governing thought."** This is the most common pyramid failure: well-supported sub-claims that do not aggregate to the headline.

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] SCQA fits on one page and the Question is sharply worded
- [ ] Governing thought is a debatable, specific answer in one sentence
- [ ] 3 (max 4) supporting arguments, MECE and parallel in form
- [ ] Each supporting argument is load-bearing (cutting it breaks the governing thought)
- [ ] Evidence sits at the third level — not in the supporting arguments themselves
- [ ] Day-1 slide written and tested for "if this is all they read, can they decide?"
- [ ] Three rounds of "but why" survived
- [ ] All four narrative lengths (30s / 2min / 10min / 60min) prepared
- [ ] Top two hostile questions per supporting argument prepared with answers

## Common Failure Modes

- **Chronological storytelling.** Walking the audience through how the team did the work. Audiences do not care about the journey; they care about the answer.
- **Topic headlines instead of message headlines.** Slide titles like "Market Analysis" instead of "The market is growing 18% but our share is declining."
- **Five supporting arguments instead of three.** Five points cannot be remembered. Force the merge.
- **Hedging the governing thought.** Phrases like "we may want to consider exploring" are not recommendations. Take a position.
- **No Day-1 slide.** Asking the audience to wait 25 slides to understand the ask.
- **Defensive tone.** Building the narrative as if pre-apologizing for the recommendation. The narrative should be confident; the analyst should be open to being wrong.

## Deliverable

A one-page SCQA, a three-level pyramid (governing thought / supporting arguments / evidence), a Day-1 slide, four prepared narrative lengths (30s / 2min / 10min / 60min), and a hostile-question prep sheet.
