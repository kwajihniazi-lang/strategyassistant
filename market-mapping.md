# Market Mapping

## Purpose

Produce a defensible answer to three questions: **how big is this market, how should we cut it, and where is value being left on the table?**

The output is a map a CFO would underwrite an investment against — not a TAM slide built to justify a number already chosen.

## Governing Principle

**Build the market bottom-up. Cross-check it top-down. Discount both.**

A senior associate never trusts a single sizing method. The triangulation is the rigor.

## The Three Layers Every Market Map Must Contain

1. **Size** — How much demand exists, who pays for it, and how it is changing
2. **Structure** — How the demand splits into segments with distinct economics and behavior
3. **Spaces** — Where supply does not yet meet demand well

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Define the market by the job, not the product

Most market sizing fails at the definition step. Avoid product-shaped definitions ("the market for X software"). Use a job-shaped definition: **who is hiring what, to do what, in what context?**

Write the market definition as one sentence: *"[Buyer type] in [context] who need to [job-to-be-done], today served by [current solutions]."*

If the sentence does not fit, the definition is too broad.

### Step 2 — Size top-down (sanity anchor)

Pull two or three independent industry sources (analyst reports, regulatory filings, trade association data). Reconcile:

- What is the headline market size?
- What is the growth rate, and is it accelerating, decelerating, or steady?
- What scope of activity does each source actually count? (They never agree — read the methodology footnotes.)

Top-down gives you the order of magnitude, nothing more. Do not anchor strategy on it.

### Step 3 — Size bottom-up (the real number)

Build the market from primary units:

- **Volume driver:** how many buyers exist? (companies, households, transactions)
- **Frequency driver:** how often do they buy?
- **Price driver:** what do they pay per unit?
- **Mix:** how does the above vary by segment?

Volume × Frequency × Price = bottom-up TAM. Do this segment by segment and roll up.

If bottom-up and top-down differ by more than ~30%, do not reconcile by averaging. Investigate the gap — that is where the real insight lives.

### Step 4 — Apply the TAM / SAM / SOM discipline

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** all demand for the job, globally, all buyers
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** the slice we could realistically reach with our model, geography, and channel
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** the slice we can realistically capture in 3–5 years given competition and capability

Most strategies fail because the team commits to TAM economics with SOM reality. Be explicit about which number is which.

### Step 5 — Segment MECE, by behavior not demographics

Cut the market by the variable that most predicts buying behavior. Test segmentation cuts in this order — pick the first one that produces 3–7 segments with distinct economics:

1. **Job-to-be-done** (the problem they are solving)
2. **Willingness-to-pay** (price tier)
3. **Buying process** (how they decide)
4. **Use case intensity** (light / medium / heavy)
5. **Channel** (how they want to be reached)

Demographics last, only as a tiebreaker. A segmentation by company size or industry alone is almost always a weak cut.

For each segment, build a one-line profile:
- Size (revenue or buyer count)
- Growth rate
- Profitability of serving them
- Concentration (few big buyers vs. many small)
- Switching behavior (sticky vs. promiscuous)

### Step 6 — Find the white space

For each segment, plot a **demand vs. supply quality** map:

- **Demand axis:** how much unmet need exists? (survey data, job-board signals, complaint volume, search trends)
- **Supply axis:** how well does the current solution set serve it? (NPS, churn, feature gap analysis)

Four quadrants emerge:
- **Crowded & served** — efficient market, hard to enter
- **Crowded & underserved** — opportunity if you can deliver differently
- **Sparse & low-need** — ignore
- **Sparse & high-need** — the white space; investigate why nobody serves it

The fourth quadrant is either a goldmine or a graveyard. The question is always: *why has no one done this yet, and is that reason still valid?*

### Step 7 — Stress-test growth assumptions

For each priority segment, decompose growth into:

- **Volume growth** (more buyers entering the market)
- **Frequency growth** (existing buyers buying more often)
- **Price growth** (price realization)
- **Mix shift** (movement toward higher-value tiers)

If the segment story relies on all four going up simultaneously, it is fiction. Real markets have trade-offs.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The published market size is wrong by a factor of 2."** What does the bottom-up build tell us that the analyst report misses?
2. **"The biggest segment is not the most attractive."** Which segment has the best economics, not just the most revenue?
3. **"The white space is empty for a reason."** What structural barrier (regulatory, distribution, behavioral) has kept incumbents out, and is it eroding?
4. **"The growth rate masks a mix shift."** Is the market growing, or are buyers migrating from one segment to another?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Top-down and bottom-up sizing reconciled (or the gap explicitly explained)
- [ ] Market defined by job, not product
- [ ] Segmentation produces MECE cuts with distinct economics — not overlapping personas
- [ ] TAM, SAM, SOM are clearly labeled and not used interchangeably
- [ ] Every white-space claim is supported by a demand signal AND a supply gap
- [ ] Growth decomposition shows volume, frequency, price, and mix separately

## Common Failure Modes

- **Self-serving TAM.** Sizing the market to justify the strategy already chosen. Senior associates size the market first, choose the strategy second.
- **Demographic segmentation.** Cutting by industry vertical or company size when behavior is the better predictor.
- **Static map.** Sizing today's market without modeling how segments will resize in 3–5 years.
- **White space without a "why now."** If the gap has existed for a decade, something is keeping it open. Find that thing.

## Deliverable

A one-page market map (TAM / SAM / SOM with both sizing methods reconciled), a segment economics table (3–7 segments, MECE), a white-space quadrant chart, and a 3–5 year growth decomposition for priority segments.
