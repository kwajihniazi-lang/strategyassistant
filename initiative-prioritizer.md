# Initiative Prioritizer

## Purpose

Reduce a list of strategic initiatives to the 3–5 that, executed well, will deliver most of the value — and put them in the right order.

The output is not a ranked list. It is a defensible prioritization, a stated capacity envelope, and an explicit "stop doing" list.

## Governing Principle

**Strategy is what you choose NOT to do. If the prioritization output does not have a 'kill' list, it is not prioritization — it is a wish list.**

Senior associates measure prioritization quality by what gets cut, not by what survives.

## The Three Disciplines of Prioritization

1. **Score on the right axes** — impact and feasibility, sized properly, not gut-felt
2. **Respect capacity** — the organization can run a finite number of initiatives in parallel
3. **Sequence for compounding** — early wins fund later moves; dependencies dictate order

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Normalize the candidate list

Most candidate lists arrive as a mess of different scopes — some are projects, some are themes, some are tactics. Before scoring, normalize:

- **One sentence per initiative:** "[Action verb] [object] [outcome] by [timeframe]"
- **Same scope:** each initiative should be roughly comparable in size (a 6-week pilot is not the same as a 3-year transformation)
- **De-duplicate:** merge initiatives that are 80%+ overlapping
- **Decompose:** break any initiative that is really a portfolio of 5 things

A clean candidate list is usually 15–30 items. If the list is 50+, the scope discipline has failed.

### Step 2 — Score impact (rigorously, not directionally)

For each initiative, estimate impact across a consistent set of dimensions. Use real numbers wherever possible.

| Dimension | Question | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue impact | Incremental revenue at year 2 | $ or % |
| Margin impact | Incremental EBITDA at year 2 | $ or pts |
| Strategic optionality | Does this open future moves? | H/M/L + rationale |
| Risk reduction | Does this reduce a downside scenario? | $ or qualitative |
| Time-to-value | When does meaningful impact land? | Months |

Force ranges, not point estimates: low / base / high. The width of the range is a signal — wider range = lower confidence in the impact claim.

### Step 3 — Score feasibility honestly

The most common prioritization failure is overestimating feasibility. Score on:

| Dimension | Question | Scale |
|---|---|---|
| Capability fit | Do we have the skills, or must we hire/build? | 1–5 |
| Capital requirement | What does this cost vs. our envelope? | $ |
| Organizational complexity | How many functions / regions must align? | Count |
| Dependency risk | What must happen first for this to work? | Listed |
| Execution time | How long until completion at sustainable pace? | Months |

Feasibility scores should be challenged by a skeptic — ideally someone who has tried similar initiatives in the past. The team running the initiative is structurally biased to overestimate feasibility.

### Step 4 — Plot on the impact-feasibility 2x2

Map every initiative on a 2x2 with bubble size = capital required.

- **High impact × High feasibility (do now):** these are the priority initiatives. Usually 3–6 candidates.
- **High impact × Low feasibility (invest to enable):** these are the capability bets. Pick at most 1–2.
- **Low impact × High feasibility (delegate or batch):** small wins; do them, but don't let them consume strategic attention.
- **Low impact × Low feasibility (kill):** must be explicitly killed and named. This is the "stop doing" list.

The 2x2 alone is not the answer — it is the input to sequencing.

### Step 5 — Apply the capacity constraint

The organization has a finite number of "strategic slots." Be brutal about how many actually exist.

A useful rule of thumb for the top of the house:
- **3 transformational initiatives** running in parallel is the upper limit
- **5–7 operational initiatives** can run concurrently if well-resourced
- **Any number of departmental initiatives** below that, but they should not compete for executive attention

If the prioritized list exceeds the slot count, more initiatives must be cut. Do not move to sequencing until the list fits within capacity.

### Step 6 — Sequence using three rules

- **Rule 1 — Dependency first:** if Initiative B requires Initiative A's output, A must precede B. Map dependencies explicitly.
- **Rule 2 — Cash-generators before cash-consumers:** earlier wins fund later bets. A self-funding sequence is more defensible than a "trust us, returns come later" sequence.
- **Rule 3 — Capability-builders before capability-consumers:** if multiple initiatives require the same new capability (e.g., a data platform, a new sales motion), build it once, early.

Plot the sequence on a 24-month roadmap with quarter-by-quarter resourcing.

### Step 7 — Define the kill criteria, in advance

For each prioritized initiative, define before kickoff:

- **Success milestone at month 3** (early signal of viability)
- **Success milestone at month 6** (commercial proof point)
- **Kill threshold** — the result that triggers shutdown, named in advance

Pre-committed kill criteria are how organizations avoid the sunk-cost trap. Without them, every initiative survives every review.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

1. **"The team's top priority is not actually the highest-value initiative."** Test the ranking against the impact data — is the ordering driven by analysis or by who advocated loudest?
2. **"Initiatives are bundled to avoid hard choices."** Check if items have been combined to dodge prioritization rather than because they are operationally linked.
3. **"Feasibility is being scored by the people who proposed the initiatives."** Apply external review.
4. **"The organization is already over capacity."** What is currently in flight that nobody has named as 'in flight'?

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] Candidate list normalized to comparable scope and de-duplicated
- [ ] Impact scored with ranges (low / base / high), not point estimates
- [ ] Feasibility scored by an independent challenger, not just the proposer
- [ ] 2x2 produced with bubble size = capital
- [ ] Final list fits within the organization's stated capacity slots
- [ ] Dependencies mapped before sequencing
- [ ] Explicit kill list named and communicated
- [ ] Pre-committed kill criteria written for every priority initiative

## Common Failure Modes

- **Ranking without cutting.** Producing a sorted list of 30 items is not prioritization.
- **Strategic cowardice on the kill list.** "We'll deprioritize" is not the same as "we will not do this." Use the harder word.
- **Sequencing by enthusiasm.** Putting the most exciting initiative first regardless of dependencies or capability readiness.
- **Ignoring the run-rate.** Forgetting that the organization is already executing — strategic initiatives compete for capacity with the day job.
- **No kill criteria.** Initiatives that cannot be killed cannot be prioritized; they are commitments dressed as choices.

## Deliverable

A normalized initiative register, scored impact and feasibility ranges, an impact-feasibility 2x2, a 24-month sequenced roadmap respecting capacity, an explicit kill list, and pre-committed kill criteria for every priority initiative.
