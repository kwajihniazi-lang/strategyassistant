# Assumption Audit

## Purpose

Identify the load-bearing assumptions underneath a strategy, grade the evidence behind each, and force the team to either upgrade the evidence or downgrade the confidence.

Most failed strategies trace to two or three wrong assumptions that nobody had explicitly stated. This skill makes them explicit.

## Governing Principle

**A strategy is only as strong as its weakest unexamined assumption.**

The senior associate's job is to find the assumption the team is most certain about — and least able to defend.

## The Three Categories of Strategic Assumption

Every strategy rests on three types of belief. Audit all three.

1. **Market assumptions** — about demand, customers, willingness to pay, and how the market will evolve
2. **Capability assumptions** — about what we (and competitors) can actually do, build, or deliver
3. **Execution assumptions** — about timing, cost, capital, talent, and organizational capacity

A strategy that fails one of these usually fails all three. They are correlated, not independent.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Surface the implicit assumptions

Take the strategy document, recommendation deck, or business case and convert every confident-sounding statement into an explicit "we assume that..." sentence.

Examples of language that hides assumptions:
- "Customers want..." → *we assume customers want X, at price Y, in volume Z*
- "The market will grow..." → *we assume CAGR of N% sustained over M years driven by [reason]*
- "We can capture..." → *we assume we can win N% share in M months, against competitor response of [type]*
- "Competitors won't..." → *we assume competitor X does not respond with [specific move] within [timeframe]*

Aim for 20–40 surfaced assumptions on a typical strategy. Fewer than 15 means the audit is incomplete.

### Step 2 — Score each assumption on the 2x2

Plot every assumption on **importance × confidence**:

- **Importance:** if this assumption is wrong, how much does the strategy break? (High = strategy fails / Low = minor adjustment)
- **Confidence:** how strong is the evidence base? (High = direct, recent, repeated data / Low = anecdote, analogy, or assertion)

Four quadrants result:

- **High importance × High confidence:** the foundation. Verify once more, then trust.
- **High importance × Low confidence:** the danger zone. THIS IS THE ENTIRE POINT OF THE AUDIT.
- **Low importance × High confidence:** background. Move on.
- **Low importance × Low confidence:** noise. Ignore.

Almost all strategic failures live in the High-Importance / Low-Confidence quadrant. Force the team to look at it directly.

### Step 3 — Grade the evidence on each load-bearing assumption

For every assumption in the danger quadrant, score evidence quality:

| Grade | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Multiple direct, recent, primary sources | Last quarter's customer data, controlled experiments |
| **B** | One direct primary source, or multiple secondary | A solid customer survey; analyst consensus |
| **C** | Analogy or pattern from a different context | "It worked for [other company]" |
| **D** | Internal expert opinion without external validation | "Our head of sales believes..." |
| **F** | Assertion with no evidence | "Everyone knows that..." |

Any High-Importance assumption graded C or below is an unacceptable foundation for capital commitment. Either upgrade the evidence or reduce the bet.

### Step 4 — Run the pre-mortem

Project forward 24 months. Assume the strategy has failed badly. Working backward, write the post-mortem.

Specifically, force the team to answer:

- Which assumption was wrong?
- What signal did we miss or dismiss?
- What were we collectively unwilling to consider?
- Who outside the team would have caught it?

The pre-mortem typically surfaces 3–5 assumptions the audit missed. It works because hindsight, even imagined, defeats motivated reasoning.

### Step 5 — Identify the kill-shot questions

For each load-bearing assumption, define the single test that would most efficiently confirm or kill it. Good kill-shot tests share three properties:

- **Cheap:** answerable in days or weeks, not quarters
- **Decisive:** the result clearly resolves the assumption either way
- **Pre-committed:** the team agrees in advance what action a "fail" result triggers

If the kill-shot test cannot be designed, the assumption is probably untestable — and the strategy is operating on faith. Name it as such.

### Step 6 — Translate audit findings into strategy adjustments

The audit must change the strategy, or it was theater. Three legitimate outcomes:

1. **Proceed:** all load-bearing assumptions are A or B grade
2. **Proceed with sequencing:** test the C-grade assumptions first, defer capital commitment until results land
3. **Reframe:** too many load-bearing assumptions are weak; the strategy itself needs to change

If every audit ends in "proceed," the audit is captured. Pressure the process.

## Hypotheses to Pressure-Test

A senior associate runs these meta-hypotheses on the team's belief system:

1. **"The team's confidence is correlated with seniority, not evidence."** Are senior leaders' assumptions getting graded with the same rigor as junior analysts'?
2. **"The most-discussed assumption is not the most-load-bearing assumption."** What is hiding underneath the visible debate?
3. **"The team is anchored on a 24-month-old worldview."** Which assumptions were formed before the last major market change?
4. **"The 'strategic imperative' is masking an unexamined assumption."** Phrases like "we have to do this" usually hide an unstated belief about the alternative.

## Quality Checks Before Sharing

- [ ] At least 20 explicit assumptions surfaced and listed
- [ ] Every assumption scored on importance and confidence
- [ ] Every High-Importance / Low-Confidence assumption has a kill-shot test designed
- [ ] Evidence grade (A–F) assigned to every load-bearing assumption with reasoning
- [ ] Pre-mortem produced at least 3 new assumptions that the structured surface missed
- [ ] Output specifies what changes in the strategy as a result

## Common Failure Modes

- **Auditing the strategy instead of the assumptions.** The audit is about beliefs, not actions.
- **Stopping at "we should validate this."** Vague validation language is the audit failing. Define the test.
- **Ignoring the obvious.** The assumption everyone treats as "of course true" is usually the most dangerous.
- **Audit theater.** Going through the motions without the political will to act on findings. Kill the audit if leadership won't commit to changing course based on results.

## Deliverable

A surfaced assumption register (20–40 items), an importance × confidence map with the danger quadrant highlighted, evidence grades on load-bearing assumptions, designed kill-shot tests for each weak assumption, and a written pre-mortem narrative.
